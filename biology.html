Asexual Reproduction in Plants

Budding

Budding is an artificial asexual reproduction method in which a new organism develops from a bud of an existing organism. Until the new organism matures, it remains attached to the parent organism.

How does it work?

A new organism is developed from a small part of the parent's body. A bud which is formed detaches to develop into a new organism which remains attached as it grows further. It is separated from the parent organism when it matures, leaving scar tissues behind. The newly developed organism is a replica of the parent and is genetically identical.


Examples

Apple Trees

T-budding is one of the most commonly used method of apple tree propagation, where a bud rather than shoot is attached to a rootstock to make a new plant.

Roses

A bud is removed from the parent plant, and the base of the bud is inserted beneath the bark of the stem of the stock plant from which the rest of the shoot has been cut.

Citrus Trees

Budwood is a stick with several buds on it that can be cut out and used for budding. It is a common method of propagation for citrus trees.

Others

Other examples of plants that reproduce with budding include cherries, ornamental plants, peach, and plums.
